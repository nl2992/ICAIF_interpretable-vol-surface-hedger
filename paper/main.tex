\title{Interpretable Volatility-Surface Hedging with Prototype Policies}

\author{Anonymous}
\affiliation{\institution{Working draft}\country{}}

\begin{abstract}
We ask whether an \emph{interpretable, prototype-based} hedger keyed on the
\emph{volatility-surface regime} can reduce tail hedging losses relative to
Black--Scholes delta and delta--vega hedging while remaining competitive with a
black-box deep hedger. Each market state is encoded from the implied-volatility
surface (level, skew, curvature, term slope) and book/hedge-instrument Greeks,
compared against a small set of learned \emph{prototypes} ($k$-means medoids in
standardised feature space), and the hedge action is a similarity-weighted blend
of bounded per-prototype actions---so every trade is traceable to named market
regimes. Policies are trained under a cost-adjusted Conditional Value-at-Risk
(CVaR) objective with analytic gradients. On a controlled regime-switching
synthetic market the prototype hedger lowers CVaR$_{95}$ by $53\%$ vs delta and
$36\%$ vs delta--vega and edges the black box, all significant under paired
bootstrap and Wilcoxon tests. On 14 years of real SPY options (2010--2023,
$3.5$M cleaned quotes), run as bounded residuals on the delta--vega hedge, it is
\emph{significantly better than delta and far more robust than the black box}
(which overfits in-sample drift and blows up out-of-sample), and
\emph{statistically on par with delta--vega} on the pooled tail and across random
seeds (a strict walk-forward shows delta--vega is the more consistent baseline).
The contribution is interpretability and robustness relative to the black box,
not outperformance of delta--vega. Removing the CVaR term inflates
CVaR$_{95}$ from ${\sim}2.4$ to $87.6$, quantifying the value of the risk
objective. Code, data pipeline and all artifacts are released for reproducibility.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{deep hedging, volatility surface, interpretability, prototypes, CVaR}

\section{Introduction}
Classical option hedging keys off spot and scalar Greeks. Yet the \emph{shape} of
the implied-volatility surface---its skew, curvature and term structure, and how
they move across regimes---carries tail-risk information that delta and
delta--vega hedges ignore~\cite{cont2002dynamics,gatheral2006volsurface}. Deep
hedging~\cite{buehler2019deephedging,cao2021deephedging} can exploit such state
but yields opaque policies, a liability for high-stakes risk
decisions~\cite{rudin2019stop}. We study the question:
\begin{quote}
\emph{Can an interpretable prototype-based volatility-surface hedger reduce tail
hedge losses versus delta / delta--vega hedging while staying competitive with a
black-box deep hedging policy?}
\end{quote}
Our contributions are: (i) a cost-aware hedging environment with an analytic P\&L
gradient; (ii) an interpretable \emph{prototype action head} over surface regimes,
inspired by prototype classifiers~\cite{chen2019thislooks}; (iii) a rigorous
comparison against deterministic and black-box hedgers under a CVaR
objective~\cite{rockafellar2000cvar} on both a controlled synthetic market and 14
years of real SPY options; (iv) an \emph{anchored residual} formulation that keeps
learned hedging robust on real, non-martingale data; and (v) a full
interpretability and ablation suite. We find the framework is sound and notably
more robust out-of-sample than a black-box hedger, while its raw tail
outperformance over the strongest classical baseline (delta--vega) is not
statistically established on real data---an honest, reproducible result.

\section{Related Work}
\textbf{Deep hedging.} \citet{buehler2019deephedging} cast hedging as risk-measure
minimisation over a neural policy; \citet{cao2021deephedging} use RL. These are
black boxes. \textbf{Interpretable ML.} Prototype networks~\cite{chen2019thislooks}
and the case for inherently interpretable models~\cite{rudin2019stop} motivate our
action head. \textbf{Volatility surfaces.} SVI~\cite{gatheral2014svi} provides
stable, arbitrage-aware smile fits we use for the real surface. \textbf{Risk
objectives.} We optimise CVaR via the Rockafellar--Uryasev
program~\cite{rockafellar2000cvar}.

\section{Problem Setup}
We short one ATM option (the liability) and hedge it to expiry by trading the
underlying and one longer-dated ATM option, rebalancing daily. Let $h_t \in
\mathbb{R}^2$ be holdings (shares, hedge-option units). Episode P\&L telescopes to
\[
\text{PnL} = \underbrace{(V_0 - V_T)}_{\text{premium}-\text{payoff}}
 + \sum_t h_t^\top \Delta P_t - \text{costs},
\]
with proportional transaction costs on traded notional (build, rebalance,
liquidation). We maximise the cost-adjusted CVaR utility
$U = \mathbb{E}[\text{PnL}] - \lambda\,\mathrm{CVaR}_\alpha(\text{loss})$ in the
smooth Rockafellar--Uryasev form, with $\alpha=0.95$.

\section{Method}
\begin{figure}[t]\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.86\columnwidth]{architecture.png}
\caption{The prototype surface-hedger pipeline.}\label{fig:arch}
\end{figure}

\textbf{State.} Standardised features (scaler fit on training data only, no
leakage): the four surface factors (level, skew, curvature, term slope),
short/long ATM vol and term slope, realised vol, recent return, one-day level
change, and the liability/hedge-option Greeks, moneyness and time to maturity.

\textbf{Prototype policy.} Prototypes $p_k$ are $k$-means medoids in the
standardised space. For state $z$, weights $w_k = \mathrm{softmax}(-\lVert z -
p_k\rVert^2 / T)$ and the action is $a(z)=\sum_k w_k a_k$, each $a_k$ a
$\tanh$-bounded per-prototype action. Top weights and prototype actions are
exposed for every decision (Fig.~\ref{fig:arch}).

\textbf{Anchoring (real data).} On a non-martingale market a mean-seeking
objective can speculate on in-sample drift; we therefore run learned policies as
bounded residuals on the delta--vega hedge, $h = h^{\Delta V} + a(z)$, so they
remain genuine hedges.

\textbf{Training.} L-BFGS-B with \emph{analytic gradients} (backprop through the
policy and the vectorised episode P\&L; unit-tested vs finite differences), L2
regularisation, and validation-CVaR early stopping. The black-box baseline is a
one-hidden-layer MLP sharing inputs, action space, costs and objective.

\section{Data}
\textbf{Synthetic.} A regime-switching stochastic-vol $+$ jump market emitting a
parametric IV surface; it is zero-carry and jump-compensated (a martingale), so a
policy can only improve the objective by genuinely hedging. \textbf{Real.}
OptionsDX SPY end-of-day chains, 2010--2023. We keep a clean OTM smile
(crossed/stale/expired filters, IV bounds, moneyness band, OTM-only) and fit the
parametric surface per day, SVI-denoised per maturity slice~\cite{gatheral2014svi};
a static no-arbitrage audit (monotonicity/butterfly/calendar) accompanies the
data. After cleaning: $3.5$M OTM quotes over $3{,}499$ trading days.

\section{Experiments and Results}
Splits are chronological/held-out; significance uses paired bootstrap and the
Wilcoxon signed-rank test on per-episode P\&L.

\textbf{Significance.} Prototype $-$ delta $=-2.32$ (CI $[-3.25,-1.29]$,

($10.0\pm11.4$)---the central robustness finding.

\textbf{Walk-forward.} Training on all prior years and testing on each subsequent
year (Fig.~\ref{fig:wf}) gives a sterner, per-regime test. Here the prototype is
$\le$ delta--vega on CVaR$_{95}$ in only $4$ of $10$ test years: \emph{delta--vega
is the more consistent baseline}, and in the sharp COVID-2020 fold the prototype's
anchored residual \emph{added} risk ($53.5$ vs delta--vega $4.1$). The black box,
however, is catastrophic in both crisis folds (2020: $61.9$; 2022: $61.0$), while
the prototype handles 2022 ($3.8$). The robust, reproducible conclusion is thus
\emph{stability relative to the black box and rough parity with delta--vega}, not
uniform outperformance.

\section{Ablations}
On real data (consistent with synthetic): the prototype count has a sweet spot at
$K{=}8$ ($K{=}4{:}2.56$, $K{=}16{:}6.54$, $K{=}32{:}9.53$); full vs greeks-only
($2.36$) vs surface-only ($2.37$) features are comparable here; \emph{dropping the
CVaR term (mean-only) explodes CVaR$_{95}$ to $87.6$} (vs $2.38$), and removing
transaction costs gives $2.21$. The CVaR objective is thus essential.

\section{Interpretability}
\begin{figure}[t]\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{activation_timeline.png}
\caption{Prototype activation over the real test period (red band: stressed start).}
\label{fig:act}
\end{figure}
Each prototype reconstructs to a concrete IV surface and a readable hedge action,
and on real data maps to historical regimes---e.g.\ a high-level, steep-skew
prototype activating predominantly around the February-2018 ``volmageddon''
(Fig.~\ref{fig:act}). We caution that on the long real sample the learned residual
is small (activation entropy ${\approx}0.11$): the prototype largely reproduces
delta--vega, so the rich, differentiated regime behaviour is clearest on the
synthetic market. This motivates a regime-\emph{diagnostic} reading of the model.

\section{Limitations and Conclusion}
The real tail improvement over delta--vega is not statistically significant
($p=0.086$) and a strict walk-forward shows delta--vega is the more consistent
baseline (prototype better in $4/10$ years, with a COVID-2020 failure); the
significant, reproducible claims are vs delta and the robustness gap vs the black
box. On the long real sample the learned residual is small (activation entropy
${\approx}0.11$), so interpretability is clearest on synthetic data. The black-box
baseline is a small MLP without architecture search; results are single-asset
(SPY), single-liability. Within these bounds, a small, auditable set of
volatility-surface prototypes delivers hedging that is \emph{competitive with
delta--vega on tail risk, dramatically more robust out-of-sample than a black-box
deep hedger, and fully interpretable}. The CVaR objective and residual anchoring
are the enabling ingredients. Code and all artifacts are released for
reproducibility.
